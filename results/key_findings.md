# Key Findings — Ireland Hospitality Sector Analysis (2019–2023)

**Author:** Cleiton Silva  
**Data Source:** CSO Ireland — Business Demography (BRA34, BRA31, BRA35)  
**Last Updated:** 2025

---

## 1. The Scale of COVID-19 Impact

Ireland's hospitality sector entered the pandemic from a position of relative strength, with **18,929 active businesses** and **221,130 persons engaged** in 2019. The onset of COVID-19 restrictions in 2020 caused an immediate and severe contraction:

- The sector lost **63,868 jobs in a single year** — a decline of **28.9%**
- Active enterprise numbers fell by 17, suggesting many businesses temporarily suspended rather than permanently closed
- The human cost was concentrated in urban and tourism-dependent counties

**County-level breakdown of employment loss in 2020:**

| County | Jobs 2019 | Jobs 2020 | Jobs Lost | % Drop |
|--------|-----------|-----------|-----------|--------|
| Co. Dublin | 85,065 | 56,394 | -28,671 | -33.7% |
| Co. Galway | 14,239 | 9,706 | -4,533 | -31.8% |
| Co. Kerry | 10,166 | 7,053 | -3,113 | -30.6% |
| Co. Clare | 5,410 | 3,829 | -1,581 | -29.2% |
| Co. Cork | 22,818 | 17,189 | -5,629 | -24.7% |

---

## 2. The Recovery Story

The recovery that followed was both swift and uneven. By 2022 the national sector had already surpassed pre-pandemic levels, and by 2023 it reached a **new historic high**:

- **2023: 20,765 active enterprises** — up 9.7% vs 2019
- **2023: 242,791 persons engaged** — up 9.8% vs 2019

The strongest recoveries were recorded in counties with smaller hospitality bases that benefited from domestic tourism growth and lower operational costs:

| County | Employment Growth 2019–2023 |
|--------|----------------------------|
| Co. Longford | +43.6% |
| Co. Kildare | +37.5% |
| Co. Offaly | +36.7% |
| Co. Wexford | +21.0% |
| Co. Meath | +18.5% |

---

## 3. Accommodation vs Food & Beverage

The two main sub-sectors followed different trajectories during the crisis and recovery:

**Accommodation (Hotels, B&Bs — NACE I55)**
- Hit harder during lockdowns as hotels were fully closed
- Slower initial recovery due to dependence on international tourism
- Fully recovered by 2022 as international travel resumed

**Food & Beverage (Restaurants, Bars — NACE I56)**
- Showed greater resilience — takeaway and delivery kept some businesses operating
- Employed significantly more people across all years
- Faster recovery trajectory in 2021 as outdoor dining restrictions eased

---

## 4. Business Health — Birth-to-Death Ratio

One of the most significant findings of this analysis is that the sector was **already contracting before COVID-19**:

| Year | New Businesses | Closures | Ratio | Sector Health |
|------|---------------|----------|-------|---------------|
| 2019 | 1,937 | 2,151 | 0.90 | Contracting |
| 2020 | 1,813 | 2,043 | 0.89 | Contracting |
| 2021 | 821 | 1,513 | 0.54 | Severely contracting |

2021 was the worst year on record — only **821 new hospitality businesses opened** across the entire country, against **1,513 closures**. This suggests the pandemic accelerated pre-existing structural pressures rather than creating them from scratch.

---

## 5. Business Size Vulnerability

Micro businesses (fewer than 10 employees) were by far the most vulnerable segment:

- Micro enterprises represented over **90% of all closures** in every year analysed
- This reflects the reality of Ireland's hospitality landscape — dominated by small independent operators with limited financial reserves
- Larger businesses (50+ employees) showed significantly greater resilience

---

## 6. Provincial Analysis

Recovery was not uniform across Ireland's four provinces:

- **Leinster** — strongest absolute numbers driven by Dublin's scale
- **Munster** — strong recovery in Cork and Kerry as international tourism returned
- **Connacht** — Galway led recovery in the west
- **Ulster (ROI)** — Donegal, Cavan and Monaghan showed strong growth, partly driven by cross-border activity

---

## 7. Summary

Ireland's hospitality sector demonstrated remarkable resilience following an unprecedented crisis. The data tells a clear story:

1. COVID-19 caused a severe but ultimately temporary shock
2. The underlying sector was facing structural pressures before 2020
3. Recovery was faster and stronger than most economic forecasts predicted
4. The benefits of recovery were unevenly distributed — urban and tourism-dependent counties took longer to recover than smaller inland counties
5. Micro businesses remain the most vulnerable segment and require policy attention

These findings are based entirely on official CSO Ireland data and represent the real experience of thousands of hospitality businesses and their employees across the country.
